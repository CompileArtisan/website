1. Second-Nature: The idea I’m tryna sell

When you gain a lot of experience in driving a car, moving the vehicle starts becoming second-nature to you. I don’t mean your hands and legs moving on their own or just magically turning into wires which connect the car and your brain. You can still turn the wheel however you like. You can choose to floor the accelerator, or you can choose to have smooth acceleration/retardation.

The car becomes second-nature to you when you find yourself doing very trivial, routine actions effortlessly. Say you’re shifting from 2nd gear to 3rd gear, exactly after 1.5K rpm, and smoothly releasing the clutch like you’ve been doing for years. Perfecting this exact sequence of oddly specific steps is when you say moving the car is second-nature to you.

Another example of this could be you turning your steering wheel for a turn, and then you turning it back into it’s straight position. While you’re doing that, you’re also speeding up. Very coordinated, yet completely in your control.

Don’t fancy cars? The way you walk is second-nature too. Your brain has the freedom to make your legs run, walk or jog. When you are in the process of running, walking or jogging, the sequence of moving your legs back and forth is all so coordinated.

It’s the very idea that powers martial arts too. All those punches and blows coordinated by various body movements to maintain the edge you have over the opponent is what makes fighting second-nature to you.

2. First step into making a keyboard second-nature to me

For me, typing on a keyboard is second-nature to me too. I’ve been touch typing for over 7 years now, and it’s made me incredibly quick. Each finger is trained to reach out for a specific set of keys and in that process, typing a word is a sequence of key-strokes. Now, tell me a word and I can execute this sequence of key-strokes very elegantly. I hope the previous section paid off in conveying this 🙂.

3. VIM

Keyboards are not only used for inserting text. Different combinations or sequences of key-strokes can also perform different actions in different applications. The evergreen Ctrl + C and Ctrl + V will always stand as a testament to the convenience of keyboard shortcuts, over selecting text and using right-click menus. But typing text on a keyboard has never really had any connection with keyboard shortcuts. Not until I was introduced to VIM. VIM is a Modal text editor, where each mode changes what your keys do. Your keys don’t just type, they also serve as different ways of interacting with text through keybinds.

For instance, a certain key-stroke places you in insert mode, where your keyboard is for inserting text (what you normally use your keyboard for). Another key-stroke takes you to normal mode where different keys can move your cursor in different ways, and edit your text in a variety of ways. Editing text is insanely keyboard-driven and it really capitalizes on my proficiency.

4. eeeeeeeemacs

GNU Emacs (I usually over-pronounce the “E” in a rather high pitch for comedic effect) is what introduced me to the idea of a using simple text files over formats used exclusively in some complicated software. Emacs stands for Editing MACros, and it’s basically a Lisp interpreter containing a buffer and cursor system. VIM is purely for the text editing, while Emacs is an entire personal computing environment.

Emacs outshines all other editing software in approximately the same way that the noonday sun does the stars. It is not just bigger and brighter; it simply makes everything else vanish.

— Neal Stephenson

The keybinds of a no-config Emacs are very different. I use Doom Emacs with Evil Mode, which means I get the goodies of Emacs, along with the editing experience of VIM.

Configuring Emacs isn’t traditional where everything is read from a file of settings to be made. Instead, you have variables used by the text editor and you write Elisp (Emacs Lisp) code to manipulate them. Neither is finding these variables nor the Elisp code a challenge because Emacs is a self-documenting software. All documentation is baked right in. Overall, Emacs provides endless customizability and loads of fun working with text.

An example of a simple text file is .org. Org mode is what I use for writing, coding and keeping track of my dates and events. For each of these tasks, there are so many features Emacs provides you with, and given its nature, there are now infinite ways to manipulate them too.

5. Conclusion

Conclusion? Simple. All of the things I use Emacs for, is now completely second-nature to me 🙂. Doom Emacs with Evil Mode wouldn’t have been possible without the knowledge of VIM, and VIM wouldn’t have been possible had there been no opportunity to capitalize on my typing speed.
